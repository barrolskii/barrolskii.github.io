    • Rarity Systems

    An overused game mechanic

    DEC. 22, 2020

    If you've played any shooter, action RPG, or free to play game within the last decade you will have seen a colour system in the game denoting how rare an item is. Usually games follow the white, green, blue, purple, orange system but there are a few here and there that have different colours like in CS:GO. For the most part though I think that this colour rarity loot is a redundant feature in most games and should never have been included in the first place.

    The Case For Colour Rarity

    Now I don't think that the system should be removed from gaming entirely. In fact I'm well aware that there are a lot of cases where the system makes sense. Take a game like Borderlands for example which is where I was first introduced to the system. The game has a tiered weapon system where the tier of a weapon directly influences its stats and what individual components that the gun can be made out of. In my opinion a colour system like this makes sense as you can quickly come to terms with what you can expect for a weapon and what to look out for when you're playing the game.

    Another game that I think utilises the colour rarity system well is Fortnite. If you're not familiar with the guns in Fortnite they have weapons that aren't randomly generated like in Borderlands but instead have set guns. These guns will then have direct upgrades that will have at minimum better damage and better accuracy. In some cases when guns reach a high enough rarity they will have an entirely new model. This is another area where I think the colour rarity system makes sense as when you're in game you can quickly identify upgrades of weapons. This is great for a multiplayer game like Fortnite as quick decision making is key. Also it has the same benefit as Borderlands where you can quickly get familiar with how a weapon of a certain tier performs. In the case of Fortnite you can learn this far quicker than Borderlands as Fortnite has far fewer guns to choose from and the guns don't have randomly spawning parts. Every white pistol is going to act the same no matter what.

    The Case Against Colour Rarity

    So this first point I feel like a lot of people will disagree with me but I really feel strongly about this. Colour rarity should not be used for weapon or character skins. There are loads of games that do this now. CS:GO is the main offender here as they've had a system like that for years. Gears of War 4 and Gears 5 have a weapon skin and character rarity colour system. Not to mention Valorant, Fortnite, and a plethora of free to play games have these attached to their microtransactions. Now the main reason to why I think rarity systems should not be attached to skins is simply because whether or not a skin is seen as good or bad is subjective. It's up to the player to decide if they like a skin or not, not your rarity system.

    Granted there are plenty of cases where the vast majority of players will say that they like a particular weapon skin and as a community said weapon skin or character skin will be sought after. But for the most part I feel systems like that are used to artificially inflate the price of a product so the company selling these microtransaction skins can make more money. Take Valorant for example. That game releases weapon skin sets on rotation. One set that was on rotation was a space themed weapon set that was very poorly and lazily made. There was no layering or animations included in the weapon skin set. It was simply a texture wrapped around an entire weapon. For this set which only included 4 weapon skins and a knife skin was sold for roughly £70. The justification for this high price was that they're all blue rarity weapon skins so they cost more and as a bundle you're getting a slight discount than if you were to buy them individually.

    Let's use Fortnite as the next example. Fortnite sells character skins that usually max out at 2000 of their in game currency. A single highest rarity character skin that you can not earn, you have no choice but to buy it will cost you roughly £16-£20. Do all of these skins look good? No of course they don't and like I said before what looks good and bad is subject to the users preference. Now I know that an argument to this is if you don't like the skins then don't buy them or if you don't like the practice don't support it. But when a game and its community put a big emphasis on these skins that you can't earn and have to pay for, I feel is a malicious practice that shouldn't be allowed to continue. Especially with artificially inflating the value of a product by putting a different colour rarity on it.

    The other case I have against rarity systems in games is when they are included into the gameplay when it serves no purpose or it is simply included for the sake of it. My main offenders here are Far Cry and Assassins Creed. I've played Assassins Creed for a long time, the old games have set weapons that you can unlock as you get further into the game and will offer slight differences. In Assassins Creed 2 I remember some weapons would have a higher attack speed, some would have better blocking, and the end game weapons would basically be great at both. Far Cry 3 was my favourite Far Cry game (like most people) and that game had a nice arsenal of weapons that all performed different roles pretty well. I remember mostly using the bow and the AK47 but that was only because those were my favourites to use.

    There was no inclusion of rarity systems back then. Granted the system wasn't a standard back then as it was today but you didn't need it. You had the weapons stats available and if you wanted to see a gun in Far Cry did more damage or a sword in Assassins Creed then you would look at the damage and compare it to the weapon you were currently using. It was that simple. If you wanted to see if you liked using a weapon then you would go out and use it. You didn't need a colour system to declare to you that the AK is better than your pistol because of a special colour that came with it. Or that the starter sword is worse than a sword a shop is selling because the starter sword is white rarity and the shops sword is green. You knew because you used it and you looked at the stats. Adding a colour rarity system here is an empty game mechanic that adds nothing to the players experience.

    I've noticed now with the new Far Cry and Assassins Creed titles that they've added health bars and damage numbers to enemies along with these loot rarity systems. These aren't the only games to do this but it seems that adding these colour systems turns franchises into Diablo/Borderlands spins offs in different genres.

    The Bottom Line

    I'm aware that this does sound like me ranting about a system for the sake of it or that I'm ranting just because I don't like it but that's not the case and that's not the point I want you to take away from reading this. I guess the tldr of this is I think the colour rarity system is overused and not needed in most games today. Like I said at the start it does have a useful purpose and can be a great mechanic in games but from how overused it is right now most games just have it for the sake of having it or like I said to artificially inflate the price of microtransactions. I think that the rarity colour system should be in a game when it makes sense, when it is actually respected and weapons and loot in a game are accurately represented with the correct rarity or the colour of rarity quickly shows a player that they have an upgrade right in front of them. You don't always need to colour code items to show that they are rare or good. Simply showing the stats and having items that you can only unlock with a lot of in game earnable currency shows to a user that it's good. Not an orange highlight.
