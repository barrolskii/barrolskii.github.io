    An overlooked problem in gaming

    FEB. 07, 2021

    If you've played any popular game within the past three years you would've seen or been advertised a battle pass. Regardless of the games popularity or if it suits the games model, these passes are popping up everywhere. You'll have them guaranteed in any free to play battle royale to full priced AAA releases ranging from Gears 5, Call Of Duty, and now Sea Of Thieves. I've noticed a common pattern with all of these implementations of battle passes across a wide variety of games. They're all anti-consumer and everybody seems to be okay with that.

    Popularised Anti Consumerism

    So how are battle passes anti consumer? Granted at first they don't seem it. You pay a games company a small fee for their battle pass and you can unlock a lot of content from it. I'll admit the price for a lot of these passes and what you can actually get from them is really good value for money. They look like an even better value when you compare the price of the pass to individual microtransactions for skins and emotes. So if they're better than the alternative of microtransactions then how is it bad? They're bad because the passes themselves are time limited. Meaning if you don't unlock everything by a specific deadline then you can never earn that content again. Regardless if you paid for the pass or not, once its gone its gone.

    Battle passes are part of a bigger problem that is digital ownership. If you pay for a digital item do you own it? Or does the company who made it own the item and are simply letting you use it? Personally I think if you buy an item, digital or not, the consumer owns it. I won't go down that rabbit hole in this post so I'll keep to the topic of battle passes. The monetisation model of battle passes shows that you do not own the pass. You can not view a previous passes tiers when a new one is out. This would be like a company releasing an expansion pass that you can play for three months and then when they release a new expansion, regardless if you paid for the last one or not, you can no longer play what you paid for and to me that just doesn't make sense.

    The worst part about this is that every single battle pass in games does this and people seem completely fine with it. So we see more and more companies adding battle passes to their games and players buying them up without a second thought. This has caused a huge surge in these passes becoming a standard for games to include and thus brought about a model that is incredibly popular and blatantly anti consumer.

    The Deadline Is Approaching

    Another standard that you see with battle passes is the seasonal content model. At first like the battle pass model it seems really good. After a set amount of time the game will receive more content put into it which, in theory, should mean the game will continually receive patches and support over its lifetime. The only issue with these seasonal timers is that they're not hype countdowns for the next content drop. They're a deadline timer for both the players and the employees working at the company.

    Upon these seasonal timers reaching zero and the new content is put into the game you can kiss goodbye to the content of that season. If you've unlocked it all then that's great! You can still use that content in game if you choose to. But what if you didn't unlock everything? Well tough luck! You can't get any of those items any more. There's a new pass out and you should buy that instead. It doesn't matter if you bought the pass and partially completed it. The season is over and so is your chance to get the skins and emotes you wanted.

    This grinds my gears for a few reasons. Firstly is that by locking content you create a sense of panic in players. It doesn't matter if the player is enjoying your game or not. You're almost forcing them to play your game as much as possible if they want to get the digital items that are on the battle pass. I understand that an argument for this is that seasons last a long time. The only problem with that is in the big picture it doesn't matter how long the season is. When all games require you to a big chunk of time into them and almost all of them having this seasonal battle pass system implemented, it is not a case of you might lost out, it is a case that you will lose out.

    The second reason that this annoys me is that this system has no regard for the players free time. There are plenty of games coming out every year and people will want to play them. I understand that games as a whole are very competitive and standing out and keeping player retention is hard but these systems are very punishing. If a player has other hobbies that they like to balance with gaming, or they have other games they like to play, or simply something happens in their personal life that they have to dedicate their free time to instead of playing games, the timer doesn't care. If you didn't put enough time to get the items on the pass then you can't ever get them again.

    The last reason I don't like it is because it's a simple way to artificially inflate play time in a game. You can design a battle pass to take X amount of hours at minimum to complete. Regardless if the game is in a good state or not you can put out good looking items that are exclusive to the season and then create a nice little echo chamber. Oh the community is complaining about poor design decisions in the game? We don't have to put that high on the priority list, just look at the player count and the average time a player plays the game. As long as the content comes out on time and players keep buying the battle passes who cares if the quality of the product is good? Just make sure players are engaged and when the numbers start dipping then improve the game.

    An example of this happening is with Gears 5 (yes I know I go on about that game a LOT but it makes so many mistakes that you can learn from it's basically a gold mine). The game released with a battle pass system (which granted was free for all but that's not the point here) and they built the unlock system around that and the microtransaction store. So if you wanted nice looking skins then you either had to sink a lot of time into the game or cough up more money which unless you got it through game pass, already cost you £50-£60. People complained on YouTube and the Gears forums about the unlock system and how unrewarding it was but the Coalition didn't listen. They kept adding systems that artificially increased play time (like the totem system if anybody remembers how bad that was). As time went on the player base has dwindled and average monthly players on Steam peaks around 900 players. You can't see the average for Xbox and game pass as Microsoft doesn't make those numbers public but there are games from 2012 with a higher active player base. Some of them are quadruple or quintuple the size. The Coalition have changed the unlock system and the store along with a load of other systems in the game but from what I can see not to much avail (might be a different story if I could see the stats but I can only go off of Steam player numbers here and my own experience with queue times and playing the game on launch). This is a great example of if you don't value the player then people will leave and stop playing your game.

    How Battle Passes Can Be Better

    Now on paper I like the idea of a battle pass. You support the developer by giving them a small fee and they allow you to unlock a lot of content by playing the game. Being able to simply unlock content is really good as with the rise for "pay to win" systems being popular, going back to unlocking items shows people that you simply didn't just take your wallet out and get buy an item. In this case you did put some time into the game albeit unlocking things with persistence rather than skill but that's a different topic. Also dropping new content each in game season and constantly updating and adding support to the game is a great thing. As long as you support your game well then people will continue to play and support it.

    Now I think that battle passes and seasonal content could be better by making some very simple changes. If you buy the pass then you own that pass. Regardless if the season ends or not, you should be allowed to unlock the items on the pass on your time, not when the game developers say. I also think that battle passes from previous seasons should be able to be purchased after the season has ended. I would say it's okay to allow a bit of a gap between purchasing a pass. For example releasing the pass for season 1 when season 4 has been release. But overall there is nothing bad about making a battle pass purchasable when the season is over. Players who missed the season get a chance to unlock items they missed out on and the games company gets more money from these users. It's a win win really.

    So that's what I think about battle passes. With a few simple changes it could go from an anti consumer practice to a pro consumer monetisation model with long lasting benefits to a game. Hopefully we can see a system like this implemented and see the standard for battle passes shift. But sadly for now we'll have to get through the current state of battle passes and seasonal content models and demand for change. We deserve good and rewarding systems in games and we should demand for them.
